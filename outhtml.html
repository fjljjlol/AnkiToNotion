• Which cellular change is characterized by an increased number of cells?
    `{{c1::Hyperplasia}}`
    • may be a risk factor for future malignancy (e.g. endometrial hyperplasia) but not considered premalignant
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by an increased size of cells?
    `{{c1::Hypertrophy}}`
    • Extra
    • First Aid


  • `{{c1::Hypertrophy::(hypertrophy or hyperplasia)}}` involves gene activation, protein synthesis, and production of organelles
    • This results in an increased in size of cells
  • `{{c1::Hyperplasia::(hypertrophy or hyperplasia)}}` involves the controlled proliferation of stem cells and differentiated cells
    • this results in an increased in the number of cells; excessive stimulation of these cells may result in pathologic hyperplasia (can progress to dysplasia and cancer)
  • Do permanent tissues (e.g. cardiac muscle, skeletal muscle, nerve) undergo hypertrophy and/or hyperplasia?
    `{{c1::Hypertrophy, only}}`
    • cannot make new cells to undergo hyperplasia
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by a decrease in tissue mass due to decrease in size or number of cells?
    `{{c1::Atrophy}}`
    • causes include disuse, denervation, loss of blood supply, loss of hormonal stimulation, poor nutrition, aging, and mechanical pressure
    • First Aid


  • Atrophy via decrease in cell number occurs via `{{c1::apoptosis}}`
    • This is an ATP-dependent programmed cell death, responding to the presence of pro-death signals or the lack of survival signals

    • First Aid


  • Atrophy via decrease in cell size may occur via `{{c1::ubiquitin-proteasome}}` degradation of cytoskeleton
    • The Proteasome is an intracellular tagging system that allows for degradation of misfolded / unwanted / used up / damaged intermediate filaments of the cytoskeleton are "tagged" with ubiquitin and destroyed by proteasomes
    • First Aid


  • Atrophy via decrease in cell size may occur via `{{c1::autophagy}}` of cellular components
    • This process is mediated by the Beclin-1 core complex; which helps generate autophagic vacuoles that consume cellular organelles, these combine with lysozymes whose hydrolytic enzymes break down organelles for nutrients
      Autophagy is a response to a state of cellular starvation, this is recognized by Akt and results in inhibition of mTOR, relieving inhibition of autophagy
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by replacement of one cell type by another?
    `{{c1::Metaplasia}}`
    • Extra
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change typically occurs after chronic exposure to an irritant, such as gastric acid or smoke?
    `{{c1::Metaplasia}}`
    • metaplastic cells are better able to handle the new stress:
      - Repeated gastric acid causes stratified squamous epithelium of lower esophagus to be replaced with columnar epithelium + goblet cells to be able to better handle stress (Barrett' esophagus)
      - Repeated cigarette smoke causes respiratory ciliated columnar epithelium to be replaced by stratified squamous epithelium
    • First Aid


  • Metaplasia occurs via reprogramming of `{{c1::stem}}` cells, which then produce the new cell type
    • reprogramming with the goal that the new cell type can adapt to a new stress
    • First Aid


  • Is metaplasia reversible or irreversible?
    `{{c1::Reversible}}`
    • with removal of the irritant
    • First Aid


  • Under persistent stress, metaplasia can progress to dysplasia and eventually `{{c1::cancer}}`
    • e.g. Barrett's esophagus may progress to adenocarcinoma of the esophagus
    • First Aid


  • Which metaplasia carries no increased risk for malignancy?
    `{{c1::Apocrine metaplasia of breast}}`
    • seen with fibrocystic change of the breast
    • First Aid


  • Vitamin `{{c1::A}}` deficiency can result in metaplasia
    • First Aid


  • What vitamin is necessary for differentiation of specialized epithelial surfaces?
    `{{c1::Vitamin A}}`
    • e.g. conjunctiva covering the eye (deficiency causes keratomalacia)
    • First Aid


  • What musculoskeletal disorder is characterized by metaplasia of skeletal muscle to bone?
    `{{c1::Myositis ossificans}}`
    • - The non-hereditary form (myositis ossificans traumatica) is due to trauma to muscle causing bone to form
      - hereditary (myositis ossificans progressiva) is due to an autosomal dominant mutation in the activin type 1 receptor, causing endothelial cells to transform to mesenchymal cells and then to bone

      *note it is separated from the bone-its in the muscle (it is not an osteosarcoma)
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by disordered, non-neoplastic cellular growth?
    `{{c1::Dysplasia}}`
    • often arises from longstanding pathologic hyperplasia or metaplasia
    • First Aid


  • Dysplasia is a term used only with `{{c1::epithelial}}` cells
    • First Aid


  • Dysplasia most often refers to proliferation of pre-`{{c1::cancerous}}` cells
    • First Aid


  • Is dysplasia reversible or irreversible?
    `{{c1::Reversible}}`
    • with alleviation of the stressor; severe dysplasia usually progresses to carcinoma (irreversible)
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by failure of cell production during embryogenesis?
    `{{c1::Aplasia}}`
    • e.g. unilateral renal agenesis
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular change is characterized by decreased cell production during embryogenesis?
    `{{c1::Hypoplasia}}`
    • results in a relatively small organ (e.g. ovarian streak in Turner syndrome)
    • First Aid


  • Which cellular adaptation is usually preceded by persistent metaplasia or pathologic hyperplasia?
    `{{c1::Dysplasia}}`
